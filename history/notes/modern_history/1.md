## 1.1 Setting the Context: Why 18th Century India?

- **1707** — Death of Aurangzeb → Decline of Mughal Empire begins
- The Indian political structure became **fragmented**
- A **political vacuum** was created
- **Competition** arose between:
  - Indian powers (regional states)
  - European powers (Portuguese, Dutch, English/British, French)

### Europeans in India — Order of Arrival:
1. **Portuguese** — 1498 (Vasco da Gama)
2. **Dutch**
3. **English (British)**
4. **French**

> 🔑 **Key Point:** Europeans initially came for **trade and commerce**. Their political ambitions emerged gradually, especially after the decline of the Mughals. The Portuguese (1498) could not fulfill political ambitions due to the strong Mughal presence.

---

## 1.2 Characteristics of 18th Century India

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Political** | Decline & disintegration of Mughal Empire |
| **Economic** | Decline — India had ~25% of World GDP in early 18th century; later exploited |
| **Cultural** | Stagnation |
| **Social** | Fragmented & hierarchical society (caste, religion, gender divisions) |
| **External** | Invasions by foreigners (Iranians & Afghans) |
| **Transformation** | Europeans changed from traders → territory masters |

> 🔑 **Key Point:** India's social fragmentation (caste, religion, gender) prevented a **joint front** against foreign powers — this was a critical weakness exploited by the British.

> 📝 **Conclusion:** The decline of the Mughal Empire was **not a single event but a gradual process spanning ~150 years** (1707–1857).

### Why Britishers Maintained the Mughal Office Post-1803:
- Even after capturing Delhi in 1803, the British did **not** immediately abolish the Mughal Empire
- **Reason:** The Mughals were still a powerful symbol in the **common psyche** of Indians
- Removing Mughals immediately could trigger popular revolts
- So the British maintained the Mughal Emperor as a **puppet/pensioner**
- Only after the **Revolt of 1857** (in which Bahadur Shah Zafar II led the revolt from Delhi) was the Mughal Empire formally ended

## 1.5 The Later Mughals (1707–1857)

### Key Timeline for Mughals:
- **1526** — Babur founds the Mughal Empire (Battle of Panipat I)
- **1707** — Death of Aurangzeb → Later Mughals begin
- **1857** — End of Mughal Empire (Bahadur Shah Zafar II exiled)

**Period of Later Mughals:** From Bahadur Shah I to Bahadur Shah Zafar II (1707–1857)

---

### 🔶 A. Bahadur Shah I (1707–1712)

**Real name:** Muazzam (also spelled Muazzam)

#### War of Succession After Aurangzeb's Death:
Aurangzeb had three sons:
| Son | Province as Governor |
|-----|---------------------|
| **Muazzam** | Kabul |
| **Azam** | Gujarat |
| **Kam Bakhsh** | Deccan |

#### Key Battles of Succession:
| Battle | Date | Between | Result |
|--------|------|---------|--------|
| **Battle of Jajau** | 12 June 1707 | Muazzam vs. Azam | **Muazzam defeated Azam** |
| **Battle of Hyderabad (Bijapur)** | 1709 | Muazzam vs. Kam Bakhsh | **Muazzam defeated Kam Bakhsh** |

- **Muazzam** emerged victorious at age **63–65** and took the title **Bahadur Shah I**
- He is also known as **Shah Bekhhabar** (the uninformed/unaware king) — title given by medieval historian **Khafi Khan**

#### Policies of Bahadur Shah I:
- **Reversed** the religious bigotry policies of his father Aurangzeb
- Pursued **compromise and conciliation** with:
  - Rajputs
  - Marathas
  - Sikhs
  - Bundelas
  - Jats
- However, these policies were **half-hearted**:
  - Granted Marathas the right to collect **Sardeshmukhi** (1/10th tax as chieftain of Maratha territory) but **NOT Chauth**
  - **Released Shahu** (son of Sambhaji, grandson of Shivaji) along with his mother from Mughal imprisonment
  - Tried to pacify Sikhs by giving **Guru Gobind Singh Ji** high Mughal mansab (rank)
  
> 📝 **Note on Sardeshmukhi and Chauth:**
> - **Sardeshmukhi** = 1/10th tax; right of Marathas as chief of Maratha territory
> - **Chauth** = 1/4th of revenue from non-Maratha areas
> - Bahadur Shah I gave Sardeshmukhi but NOT Chauth → half-hearted appeasement

- Also **crushed the rebellion of Banda Bahadur** (Banda Singh Bahadur), who became the Sikh leader after Guru Gobind Singh Ji (the last living Sikh Guru)

> ⚠️ **UPSC Important:** Statement test — He REVERSED (not continued) Aurangzeb's policies. He gave Sardeshmukhi but NOT Chauth to Marathas.

---

### 🔶 B. Era of Powerful Nobles — A New Political Reality

After the death of Bahadur Shah I (1712), a **new development** in Mughal politics emerged:

> **Mughal nobles became direct contenders for power.** They used Mughal princes as mere pawns to capture state authority.

- The Mughal Emperor became **dependent on nobles** for his throne
- This era marked the **real beginning of Mughal decline**

---

### 🔶 C. Jahandar Shah (1712–1713)

- Became emperor with the support of **Zulfiqar Khan** (powerful noble)
- Was a **weak, pleasure-loving prince** — actual administration was in Zulfiqar Khan's hands
- Zulfiqar Khan's key actions:
  - Reversed Aurangzeb's policies
  - **Abolished Jizya** (tax on non-Muslims, re-imposed by Aurangzeb)
  - Granted Marathas both **Sardeshmukhi AND Chauth** of the Deccan
  - Introduced **Ijara System** (Revenue Farming/Contract Farming)

#### Ijara System (Revenue Farming):
- The **right to collect revenue** from a specific land/area was **auctioned to the highest bidder**
- **Positive impact:** Provided stable/fixed income to the state
- **Negative impact:** Led to **exploitation of peasants/cultivators** (the highest bidder would extract maximum taxes to recover his investment)

- Jahandar Shah's reign ended when **Farrukh Siyar** defeated him at Agra in January 1713

---

### 🔶 D. Farrukh Siyar (1713–1719)

- Became emperor with support of the **Sayyid Brothers** (Abdullah Khan and Hussain Ali Khan)
- The Sayyid Brothers were known as the **"Kingmakers"** (Wazir and Mir Bakshi)
- Farrukh Siyar was **cowardly, cruel, and faithless** — began plotting against the Sayyid Brothers
- **1719:** Sayyid Brothers, with the support of **Maratha Peshwa Balaji Vishwanath**, **deposed Farrukh Siyar**

> ⚠️ **UPSC Important:** Sayyid Brothers removed Farrukh Siyar with the help of **Marathas** (Balaji Vishwanath was Peshwa at the time)

---

### 🔶 E. The Sayyid Brothers — Kingmakers

| Person | Position |
|--------|---------|
| **Abdullah Khan** | Wazir (Prime Minister) |
| **Hussain Ali Khan** | Mir Bakshi (Paymaster General) |

- After deposing Farrukh Siyar, they installed **two phantom kings** (Rafi-ud-Darajat and Rafi-ud-Daulah) who died quickly of illness
- Then **Muhammad Shah Rangila** came to power (also with Sayyid Brothers' support)

---

### 🔶 F. Muhammad Shah Rangila (1719–1748)

**Real name:** Roshan Akhtar

- Came to throne with Sayyid Brothers' support
- His reign was the **last chance to save the Mughal Empire** — but he failed
- His character: Pleasure-loving, fickle-minded, erratic behavior; spent months in the harem

#### End of Sayyid Brothers (1720):
- Muhammad Shah, with the help of **Nizam-ul-Mulk** and **Muhammad Amin Khan**, conspired against the Sayyid Brothers
- **1720** — End of Sayyid Brothers' dominance in Mughal politics

#### Key Developments During Muhammad Shah's Reign:

**1. Appointment of Nizam-ul-Mulk as Wazir (1722):**
- Nizam-ul-Mulk (also called Asaf Jah / Chin Qilich Khan) tried to reform the administration
- Got frustrated with Muhammad Shah's erratic behavior
- Left the Mughal court and went to the Deccan
- **Proclaimed independent state of Hyderabad in 1724** (practically, not officially)
- This marked the **beginning of the physical break-up of the Mughal Empire**

**2. States that broke away during his reign:**
- **Hyderabad** — Nizam-ul-Mulk
- **Bengal** — Murshid Quli Khan
- **Awadh** — offered only nominal loyalty
- **Rohilkhand** — offered only nominal loyalty

## 1.6 Invasion of Nadir Shah (1739)

| Detail | Information |
|--------|-------------|
| **Who** | Nadir Shah — ruler of Persia (Iran) |
| **Battle** | **Battle of Karnal** — February 1739 |
| **Result** | Nadir Shah decisively defeated Muhammad Shah Rangila |
| **Loot** | ~70 crore rupees worth |
| **Items Taken** | **Peacock Throne (Takht-e-Taus)**, **Koh-i-Noor Diamond** |
| **Muhammad Shah** | Imprisoned by Nadir Shah |
| **Territory Lost** | All provinces **west of River Indus** ceded to Nadir Shah |

### Impact of Nadir Shah's Invasion:
1. **Exposed the hidden weakness** of the Mughal Empire to Indian sardars and European trading companies
2. **Provided opportunity** for Marathas and foreign trading companies to fill the power vacuum
3. **Loss of northwestern defense line** — territories west of River Indus lost → opened India to further invasions from northwest
4. **Physical break-up** of Mughal Empire accelerated — Hyderabad, Bengal, Awadh became independent
5. Paved the way for **Ahmad Shah Abdali's invasions**

> 🔑 **Key Point:** After Nadir Shah's invasion, two major powers competed to fill the political vacuum — **Marathas** (Indian power) and **British** (European trading company)

---

## 1.7 Invasion of Ahmad Shah Abdali / Durrani (1748–1761)

- **Ahmad Shah Abdali** (also called **Ahmad Shah Durrani**) — Afghan ruler
- Invaded India repeatedly from **1748 to 1761**
- His invasions were a direct consequence of the weakening of India's northwestern frontier after Nadir Shah's invasion
- Culminated in the **Third Battle of Panipat (1761)** — decisive confrontation with the Marathas

---

## 1.8 Emergence of Regional States

With the disintegration of the Mughal Empire, **three types of regional states** emerged:

| Type | Examples |
|------|---------|
| **Successor States** (broke away from Mughal Empire) | Bengal (Murshid Quli Khan), Hyderabad (Nizam-ul-Mulk), Awadh (Sa'adat Khan) |
| **New/Independent States** | Mysore, Kerala |
| **Rebellious/Indigenous States** | Marathas, Rajputs, Sikhs, Jats |
