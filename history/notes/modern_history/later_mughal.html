Ijara System — Revenue Farming Explained

🏛️
Mughal State Needs Revenue
The state needs a fixed, reliable income from land tax
📢
Land Auctioned to Highest Bidder
Revenue collection rights sold through open auction
💰
Bidder Pays Fixed Amount to State
State gets stable income regardless of actual harvest
👨‍🌾
Bidder Extracts Maximum from Peasants
To profit, the bidder squeezes as much as possible from cultivators

✅ Positive Impact

Stable, predictable income for the State treasury regardless of agricultural conditions

❌ Negative Impact

Severe exploitation of peasants and cultivators — maximised extraction with no welfare concern

✅ British WIN — Systematic expansion post-Plassey 1757. Establish political control over entire India.

Why Did British Win?

Superior military organization, political cunning, naval power, divide-and-rule tactics, and crucially — India's fragmented society had no unified front (caste divisions, religious divisions, no shared identity) made it impossible to coordinate resistance.

⚠️ Exam Trap

Sardeshmukhi vs Chauth — Don't Get This Wrong

🎯 High-Frequency UPSC Trap Question

🏹 Sardeshmukhi

= 1/10th of revenue
Right as chieftain of Maratha territory.

✅ Bahadur Shah I gave this
✅ Zulfiqar Khan (under Jahandar Shah) also gave this

💰 Chauth

= 1/4th of revenue
Collected from NON-Maratha areas (protection money).

❌ Bahadur Shah I did NOT give Chauth (half-hearted!)
✅ Zulfiqar Khan gave BOTH Sardeshmukhi AND Chauth

Memory Trick: Bahadur Shah I was more restrictive — gave only the smaller Sardeshmukhi (1/10). Jahandar Shah's minister Zulfiqar Khan was trying to win Maratha support at any cost — so gave both the big AND small right. Bahadur Shah giving Chauth is a common wrong answer in MCQs.

One-Paragraph Summary

After Aurangzeb's death in 1707, the Mughal Empire entered a 150-year decline driven by weak successors, powerful nobles (Sayyid Brothers), and devastating foreign invasions — Nadir Shah (1739) and Ahmad Shah Abdali (1748–1761). As the empire physically disintegrated into successor and new regional states, a power vacuum emerged. Marathas — India's strongest power — failed at the Third Battle of Panipat (1761). Meanwhile, the British East India Company, originally traders, seized the moment at the Battle of Plassey (1757) to become political masters. By 1803, Delhi itself was under British control. This sets the stage for colonial exploitation (Unit 2), popular resistance (Unit 3), and the organized Freedom Struggle (Unit 4) — culminating in Independence on 15 August 1947.
